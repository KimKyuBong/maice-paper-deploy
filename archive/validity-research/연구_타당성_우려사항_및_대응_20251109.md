## π“ λ…Όλ¬Έ Limitations μ„Ήμ… μ‘μ„±μ•

### 5.2 Limitations

#### 5.2.1 Response Bias and Validity Concerns β­ μµμ°μ„  λ…μ‹

This study acknowledges several significant threats to internal validity that warrant careful interpretation of the survey results:

**1. Teacher-Developer Dual Role and Non-Anonymous Design**

The lead researcher served dual roles as both the classroom teacher and the system developer. Students were aware of this relationship, which may have influenced their survey responses in several ways:

- **Social Desirability Bias**: Students may have reported higher satisfaction levels to please the teacher/developer
- **Demand Characteristics**: Students may have inferred that the study favored the Socratic (B) mode and responded accordingly
- **Power Dynamics**: The teacher-student relationship may have discouraged critical feedback
- **Non-anonymous Responses**: Student email addresses were collected for data linkage purposes (see justification below)

**Justification for Non-Anonymous Data Collection**:

Despite the risk of response bias, identifiable data collection was essential for the research design:

1. **Mode Assignment Tracking**: To verify which students used A-mode vs. B-mode, matching survey responses to actual usage data
2. **Cross-validation with Objective Measures**: To link survey responses with:
   - Teacher evaluation scores (external validity check)
   - AI evaluation scores (objective learning outcomes)
   - Session usage patterns (behavioral evidence)
3. **Pre-Post Comparison**: To track individual learning trajectories over time
4. **Triangulation Capability**: **The ability to match survey data with objective performance data was critical for addressing the very bias concerns that non-anonymity created**

**This represents a methodological trade-off**: Non-anonymity introduced response bias risk, but enabled robust triangulation that ultimately strengthened validity more than anonymity alone would have.

**Alternative Considered**: Coded identifiers could have provided pseudo-anonymity, but:
- Required additional infrastructure beyond study scope
- Still traceable by the teacher in small classroom context
- Would not meaningfully reduce perceived power dynamics

**Evidence of Bias Impact**:
- High overall satisfaction (M=4.32/5.0) may partially reflect social desirability
- B-mode preference (60.0%) may be inflated by perceived research expectations
- Overwhelmingly positive qualitative responses may indicate evaluation apprehension

**2. Mitigation and Triangulation Efforts**

To address these concerns, we employed several strategies:

**Triangulation with Objective Data** β­:
- **Teacher evaluation scores** (N=280 sessions): Independent assessment of learning outcomes
- **AI evaluation scores** (3 models): Objective measurement of mathematical reasoning
- **Usage behavior data**: Actual mode selection patterns vs. stated preferences
- **Session duration**: Genuine engagement levels vs. reported satisfaction

**Internal Consistency Checks**:
- Standard deviations indicate thoughtful responses (SD=0.64~1.30 across items)
- Critical feedback present (11 mentions of verbosity, 6 of circular reasoning)
- Contra-hypothesis responses (27.5% preferred A-mode despite B-mode emphasis)
- Not all items scored 5.0 (range: 3.80~4.85)

**3. Evidence Supporting Validity Despite Bias**

Several findings suggest the results retain partial validity:

**Behavioral Evidence** (Objective, Unbiased):
- Students continued using the system voluntarily beyond required sessions
- Session durations increased over time (genuine engagement)
- Mode selection patterns correlated with survey preferences
- Help-seeking behavior matched reported metacognitive development

**Convergent Evidence**:
- Socratic method preference (60%) aligns with educational literature (e.g., Chi et al., 2001)
- Metacognitive gains consistent with Flavell's framework predictions
- Specific criticisms (verbosity, circular reasoning) demonstrate honest evaluation
- Lower scores on self-regulation (4.01) vs. system satisfaction (4.63) show discrimination

**Qualitative Depth**:
- Detailed, specific examples in open responses (not generic praise)
- Nuanced reasoning for mode preferences (not simply "because it's better")
- Spontaneous suggestions for improvement (evidence of critical thinking)

**4. Revised Interpretation Framework**

Given these limitations, we interpret survey results conservatively:

| Finding | Original Claim | Conservative Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|----------------------------|
| B-mode 60% | "Strong preference" | "Moderate-to-strong preference, likely inflated by 5-15%" |
| M=4.32 satisfaction | "High satisfaction" | "Moderately high satisfaction, possibly inflated by ~0.3-0.5 points" |
| Qualitative praise | "Overwhelmingly positive" | "Generally positive with notable criticisms" |

**Cross-validation with objective data (teacher/AI scores and usage patterns) provides stronger evidence for actual learning effects than self-reported satisfaction alone.**

**5. Implications for Generalizability**

These validity concerns limit generalizability:
- Results may not transfer to anonymous survey contexts
- Effects may be stronger in teacher-led vs. independent use scenarios
- Preference percentages should be interpreted as upper bounds
- **Objective learning outcomes (teacher/AI evaluations) provide more robust evidence**
