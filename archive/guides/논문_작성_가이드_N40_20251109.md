### 3.4 Data Collection (ìƒˆë¡œ ì¶”ê°€/ê°•í™”)

#### 3.4.1 Participants and Ethics

```markdown
**Participants**: 40 high school students (Grade 11, ages 16-17) 
from a specialized science high school participated in this study. 
All students were enrolled in the same mathematics course taught 
by the lead researcher.

**Ethical Considerations and Limitations**:

The study design involved several methodological constraints that 
require transparent acknowledgment:

1. **Teacher-Developer Dual Role**: The lead researcher served as 
both the classroom teacher and the system developer. Students were 
aware of this relationship, which may have influenced their survey 
responses (see Section 5.2 for detailed discussion).

2. **Non-Anonymous Data Collection**: Student email addresses were 
collected to enable data linkage across multiple sources. While this 
design choice introduced potential response bias, it was essential for:
   - Tracking mode assignments (A vs. B)
   - Linking survey responses to objective performance metrics
   - Enabling individual learning trajectory analysis
   - Cross-validating subjective and objective measures

**Justification**: We made a deliberate methodological trade-off: 
Non-anonymity increased response bias risk but enabled robust 
triangulation with objective data. **The ability to validate survey 
findings against teacher evaluations, AI assessments, and behavioral 
data ultimately strengthened validity more than anonymity alone would 
have** (see Section 3.4.3 for triangulation strategy).

Alternative approaches (e.g., coded identifiers) were considered but 
deemed insufficient in a small classroom setting where the teacher 
could still potentially identify students.

3. **Voluntary Participation**: Students were informed that:
   - Participation was voluntary and would not affect grades
   - Honest feedback was valued over positive responses
   - Critical comments were welcome and encouraged
   - The study aimed to improve the system, not validate it
```

#### 3.4.2 Data Sources

```markdown
To address the limitations of self-report data, we employed a 
multi-source data collection strategy:

| Data Source | N | Bias Risk | Purpose |
```

### 5.3 Limitations (ì†”ì§í•˜ê³  ìƒì„¸í•˜ê²Œ)

```markdown
5.3 Limitations

This study has several important limitations that affect the 
interpretation and generalizability of findings.

#### 5.3.1 Response Bias and Survey Validity

**Methodological Constraints**:

Our survey data are subject to multiple sources of potential bias:

1. **Teacher-Developer Dual Role**: The lead researcher served as 
both the classroom teacher and system developer. Students were aware 
of this relationship, creating conditions for:
   - **Social desirability bias**: Students may have inflated 
     satisfaction to please the teacher/developer
   - **Demand characteristics**: Students may have inferred that 
     the study favored Socratic mode and responded accordingly
   - **Power dynamics**: Teacher-student relationship may have 
     discouraged critical feedback

2. **Non-Anonymous Data Collection**: Student email addresses were 
collected for data linkage purposes, potentially reducing response 
honesty despite assurances that feedback would not affect grades.

**Expected Impact**:
- Satisfaction scores (M=4.32) may be inflated by ~0.3-0.5 points
- Socratic mode preference (60%) likely overstates true preference 
  by ~5-15 percentage points (conservative estimate: 50-55%)
- Qualitative responses may under-represent critical perspectives

**Evidence of Bias**:
- High overall ratings despite system limitations
- Overwhelmingly positive open-ended responses
- Possible reluctance to fully critique teacher's work

**Justification for Design**:

Despite bias risks, non-anonymous data collection was methodologically 
necessary to:
- Link survey responses to mode assignments (A vs. B)
- Cross-validate with teacher evaluations and AI assessments
- Track individual learning trajectories
- Enable the very triangulation that addresses bias concerns

**This represents a deliberate trade-off**: We accepted response bias 
risk to gain robust cross-validation capability. Paradoxically, the 
non-anonymous design that created bias also enabled us to detect and 
account for it through objective data.

**Mitigation Strategies**:

1. **Triangulation**: Primary reliance on objective measures (teacher 
   and AI evaluations) that are immune to self-report bias
2. **Internal consistency checks**: Analysis of response patterns, 
   standard deviations, and contra-hypothesis responses
3. **Conservative interpretation**: Systematic downward adjustment of 
   stated preferences and satisfaction levels
4. **Behavioral validation**: Comparison of stated preferences with 
   actual usage patterns

**Residual Concerns**:

Even with mitigation, some uncertainty remains. Readers should 
interpret survey findings as representing the *upper bound* of 
effectiveness and preference, with objective performance data 
providing more reliable estimates.

#### 5.3.2 Generalizability

**Limited Population**:
- Single school (specialized science high school)
- Single grade level (Grade 11)
- Single topic (mathematical induction)
- Single teacher-researcher
- Short duration (~2-3 weeks)

**Implications**:
- Results may not transfer to other student populations
- Effects may be specific to mathematical induction
- Teacher-led implementation may differ from independent use
- Long-term effects unknown

#### 5.3.3 Causality

This quasi-experimental field study lacks full experimental control:
- No random assignment to conditions
- Potential confounds with teacher presence
- Self-selection bias in engagement levels
```
